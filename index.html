Welcome to Avocado-nomics! This case study demonstrates how to combine historical sales data, pricing optimization, and distribution strategy to maximize revenue. We'll walk through the complete data science pipeline from descriptive analytics through prescriptive optimization.
The fundamental question: How much is too much for an avocado? This isn't just about pricing - it's about understanding the delicate balance between price and demand. Set it too high, and customers walk away. Set it too low, and you leave money on the table.
This slide illustrates the classic pricing dilemma. Too high: lose customers. Too low: lose profit. The sweet spot exists, but finding it requires data-driven decision making. This is where analytics becomes essential.
Finding optimal price requires the perfect blend of three types of analytics: Descriptive (understanding past trends), Predictive (forecasting future demand), and Prescriptive (determining the best course of action). Like making guacamole, you need the right blend of ingredients.
Our journey has 5 parts: (1) Understanding the Great Avocado Dilemma, (2) Unpacking our data's secrets, (3) Predicting customer behavior, (4) Optimizing for revenue, and (5) Seeing the big payoff. Each builds on the previous step.
Section 1: The supplier's challenge. We have avocados to distribute across multiple regions. Each region has different demand characteristics. How do we price and allocate to maximize revenue while accounting for transportation and waste costs?
Our goal is clear: develop a complete data science pipeline for pricing and distributing avocados to maximize revenue. This means analyzing historical data, building predictive models, and creating optimization strategies.
The three-stage pipeline: (1) Descriptive - understand past trends like seasonality and regional preferences, (2) Predictive - forecast future demand based on price and other factors, (3) Prescriptive - find the best course of action to maximize revenue.
Section 2: Time to explore our data. We have historical sales data from the Hass Avocado Board. What patterns can we find? What insights are hidden in the numbers? This is the descriptive analytics phase.
Our data contains three key dimensions: calendar date (for seasonal patterns), product information (the avocados themselves), and region (where they're sold). These combine to give us a complete picture of the market.
Aha! We see predictable peaks during Super Bowl season in February and Cinco de Mayo in May. These seasonal patterns are crucial for forecasting and planning. People love their guacamole during these events!
Regional differences are striking. The West coast buys significantly more avocados on average - nearly 3 times as much as some other regions. This geographic variation means we need region-specific pricing and allocation strategies.
This visualization shows the fundamental tradeoff: on one side, high prices with concerned customers and declining sales; on the other, low prices with happy customers but insufficient profit. The balance point is what we're seeking.
Section 3: Now we build our predictive model. Using machine learning, we'll forecast demand based on price, region, year, and seasonality. This is the predictive analytics phase that bridges description and prescription.
Our predictive model learns patterns from historical data - specifically how price, region, and season affect demand. Once trained, it can predict future avocado demand for different pricing scenarios. This becomes a crucial component of our optimization model.
Excellent! Our model achieves an R-squared of 0.90, meaning it explains 90% of the variance in avocado demand. This high accuracy gives us confidence that our predictions will be reliable when we use them in the optimization phase.
Section 4: Time to craft our strategy! Now we take our predictive model and embed it in a mathematical optimization framework. We'll determine optimal prices and supply quantities for each region to maximize total net revenue.
Mathematical optimization is a method that takes a goal (maximize revenue), decision levers (price and supply per region), and rules (budget constraints, transport costs, waste costs) to find the absolute best possible solution. It's prescriptive analytics at its finest.
Our optimization recipe has three ingredients: (1) The Goal - maximize net revenue, (2) The Levers - price and supply for each region (what we can control), (3) The Rules - constraints like inventory limits, transport costs, and wastage costs.
Section 5: The results are in! Let's see what our optimization model recommends and how much value it creates compared to historical pricing and allocation decisions.
Here are the optimal prices by region: $2.00 for West, SouthCentral, and Northeast (high-demand regions), $1.51 for Midsouth, and $1.20 for Plains. Notice how the model sets higher prices in regions with stronger demand and lower transport costs.
The bottom line: $42.5 million in optimal net revenue. This represents the best possible outcome given our constraints, predicted demand, and cost structure. But how does this compare to actual historical performance?
Incredible! The optimized strategy yields a 35% increase in net revenue compared to actual historical results. This isn't just theoretical - it represents real money left on the table with non-optimized pricing and allocation decisions.
Let's recap the complete journey: Data (historical sales) → Insights (patterns in seasonality, regions, price sensitivity) → Strategy (optimized pricing and allocation). This is the power of combining analytics and optimization.
The data science pipeline in action: raw data transformed into actionable insights, which inform strategic decisions. This framework applies far beyond avocados - it's the blueprint for data-driven decision making in any domain.
